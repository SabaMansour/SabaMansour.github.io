Projects

Solving the two-dimensional convection problem on unstructured triangular meshes using C++

Sep. 2021 – Jan. 2022


Unstructured meshes are meshes with general connectivity whose structure is arbitrary and therefore the connectivity of elements must be defined and stored. These meshes are typically used when meshing complex geometries prior to conducting Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) studies. In this project, a convection problem was solved on unstructured triangular meshes. Where the desired quantity, temperature, provided a rigid body rotation about the origin with a period of 2s. In order to reach a second-order accurate solution to the problem in time and space, the following items were addressed. First, mesh connectivity was validated. Second, by solving the least-squares problem with the help of normal equation (NE) and modified Gram-Schmidt (MGS), a QR factorization method, the solution was reconstructed, and the first-order accuracy of the solution gradient was proved. The obtained results suggested that for this problem, owing to the low condition number of the coefficients matrix in the least-squares problem, NE and MGS produce the same results. Thirdly, exact and numerical flux integrals were computed and compared. It was observed that, as expected, the calculated flux integral was third-order accurate. Then, using the second-order Runge-Kutta method, the solution is advanced in time to reach any desired time. Finally, the contours of the solution at 0.5s and 1.5s were plotted for the fine mesh in order to observe the rigid body rotation.


Implementing the direct (LU decomposition) method and Krylov subspace (generalized minimal residual) method for solving a large system of equations in C++

Sep. 2021 – Jan. 2022


One of the biggest problems of associated with CFD studies at an industrial scale is high computational costs and, therefore, unbearable run times. When solving equations with direct solve methods, such as Gauss-Elimination, the required operations are of m raised to the 3rd power, where m is the number of unknowns. The matrices arising from a CFD simulation are typically large sparse matrices. Thus, iterative methods such as Krylov subspace methods are used to solve such matrices efficiently, i.e., with significantly less computational cost. In this project, a large system of equations arising from a CFD simulation was solved using both direct and a Krylov subspace method, the GMRES method, in order to examine its superiority over the direct solve method. The physical problem of this project involved the thermal development of flow in a channel in which the velocity was already fully developed. The implicit finite volume method was used for the discretization of this problem. The achieved results suggested that applying the unpreconditioned and preconditioned GMRES method result in an enormous 99.4% and 99.6% reduction of run time as compared to the direct solve, respectively.


Examination of the performance of Large Eddy Simulation models using Direct Numerical Simulation data

Sep. 2021 – Jan. 2022


Large Eddy Simulations (LES) is a mathematical model for studying turbulence and turbulent flows used in computational fluid dynamics. In this project, the data for LES using different filter functions, namely Box filter, Sharp spectral (cutoff) filter, and Gaussian filter, were constructed from Direct Numerical Simulation (DNS) data at first. Then the exact and modeled Sub-Grid Scale (SGS) stress tensors as well as Smagorinsky and dynamic Smagorinsky coefficients were computed. Finally, the performance of the LES model were be examined.


Solving the Two-dimensional Incompressible Laminar Navier-Stokes Equations using C++

Jan. 2021 – Jun. 2021


A framework for solving the two-dimensional incompressible laminar Navier-Stokes equations was established from scratch in C++. A lid-driven cavity flow was solved in which the top wall of a rectangular cavity moves horizontally. A SIMPLE algorithm along with finite-volume discretization was developed in which the momentum and pressure correction equations were solved by Gauss-Seidel scheme. Collocated grid arrangement was utilized for implementation of Rhie-Chow interpolation to compute the residual of continuity equation for inner control volumes. The results were verified by solving the same problem with the equations of stream function and vorticity.


Modelling Flow simulation over an asymmetric Jakowski airfoil

Sep. 2020 – Jan. 2021


The flow around a lifting airfoil is a fundamental Fluid Mechanics phenomenon. The study of lift and drag forces, streamlines, stagnation points, and pressure coefficients related to an airfoil is an essential part of understanding this phenomenon. In this project analytic and numerical investigations was carried out respectively based on ideal flow theory (using MATLAB) and inviscid/viscid incompressible flow (using ANSYS Fluent). The results were compared to assess the accuracy of ideal flow theory assumptions for different angles of attack.


Designing a zero energy building's energy and HVAC systems

Jan. 2020 – Jun. 2020


The heating, ventilation and air cooling (HVAC) system, which is a huge system requiring great power sources,is an inseparable part of any building's design. In this project, a complete system of a building was precisely designed and analyzed. All the building requirements including heating and cooling loads as well as electricity consumption were taken into account. The proposed system successfully maintained the temperature between 22 and 26 centigrades (71-79 degrees Farenheit) and the relative humidity between 20% and 40% all year long for a buiding in the city of Tabriz, Iran. In order for the proposed system to be zero-energy, the building's required electricity was supplied with a renewable energy source. For this purpose, wind turbines and photovoltaic panels were compared based on initial cost and photovoltaic panels were ultimately chosen.
